**3/**
Your resolution didn't die because you're lazy. It died because it was built to fail:

"Get in shape" is a wish, not a plan. It ran on motivation, which is gone by week 3. And one missed day made the whole thing feel pointless.

**4/**

## LinkedIn post

We hit the exact midpoint of 2026 today.

It's a strange little moment. Most of us set some kind of goal in January, personal or professional, and most of us let it quietly go by spring. The reflex is to feel behind.

I'd push back on that framing. This isn't "behind." It's half-time. And half-time is when the people who finish strong sit down and change the plan.

Building a habit app has made one thing obvious to me: resolutions rarely fail for lack of discipline. They fail because they're designed poorly. An outcome with no system under it. Motivation that's spent by week three. A structure where missing two days feels like failing entirely, so people quit the whole thing.

The fix isn't "want it more." It's:

Anchor to who you're becoming, not the outcome: "someone who writes daily," not "finish the book."
Break it into actions small enough to do on a bad day.
Track consistency instead of perfection, and let every month start clean.

There's nothing magic about January 1st. Any day is a fine day one. If you dropped a goal this year, today works.
